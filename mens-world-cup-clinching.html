Two games down, who's already through?

By Danny Page

Clinched, eliminated, and decided after two rounds of the Men's World Cup group stage — every 32-team tournament from 1998 to 2022, set against 2026.

Across the seven 32-team World Cups, after two of three group games an average of 4.7 teams (15% of the field) had already clinched a knockout place and 5.0 (16%) were already out. 2026 looks different on both counts. Assuming the Elo-favoured result in today's four remaining Matchday-2 games, about 8 (16%) of the 48 teams have clinched and only roughly 5 (10%) are mathematically eliminated — because the 48-team format sends two-thirds of the field forward (top two per group plus the eight best third-placed teams), so finishing third is no longer fatal. By Elo, the gap is starker still: a typical year had 35% of teams already 95%-certain to advance after two rounds; in 2026 that figure is 50%.

How many were mathematically decided

A team has clinched if no combination of final-round results — any scoreline — keeps it from a knockout place, won the group if first place is untouchable, and is eliminated if no result can get it through. For 1998–2022 a knockout place means a top-two finish; for 2026 it also includes the third-place route, so a team is only out once it cannot finish in the top three. Ties the data cannot resolve (fair play, drawing of lots) are treated against the team.

Who was a near-lock — the Elo view

Mathematical certainty is a high bar. Using World-Football Elo ratings (built from the full international match history) to simulate the final round 30,000 times, we can also count teams that were practically decided — at least 95% to advance, or to go out — even when the maths still left a door ajar. 2026 is included on the same axis; its simulation already plays out today's four games, so no projection is needed here.

How much drama survived to Matchday 3

Flip that around and it becomes a tension gauge: how many teams went into the final round with their qualification still genuinely in doubt — neither a 95% lock to advance nor a 95% lock to go out. By that measure 2002 stands alone: a draw-heavy opening (10 of 16 games drawn) with only three teams winning both their first games left most groups carrying a live three-team race into the last day. 2026 sits mid-pack — paradoxically, because the expanded field near-locks so many teams into the knockouts that fewer fates actually hang on Matchday 3, even though more teams ultimately qualify.

Why 2026 breaks the comparison

Three changes separate 2026 from everything before it. The field is 48 teams in 12 groups, not 32 in 8. The top two still advance automatically, but the eight best third-placed teams join them — so a third-place finish is usually survivable, which is why almost no one is mathematically out after two rounds. And FIFA reordered the tiebreakers: head-to-head result now precedes goal difference, reversing fifty years of practice. The charts above already fold these in; the 2026 numbers will firm up once today's four games are played.

Notes on method

Match results: the martj42 international results dataset. Groups and matchdays are reconstructed from the fixture graph (the source has no group labels). Elo follows the eloratings.net scheme (importance-weighted K, goal-difference multiplier, no home advantage at the neutral finals) over all internationals since 1872, snapshotted entering the final group round. The final round is simulated with independent-Poisson scorelines tuned so each match's implied win expectation matches its Elo expectation (mean 2.6 goals). Tiebreakers: 1998–2022 use points → overall goal difference → goals → head-to-head; 2026 uses points → head-to-head → overall goal difference → goals, with the eight best third-placed teams advancing.
